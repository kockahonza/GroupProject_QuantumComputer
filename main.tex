\begin{abstract}
        Quantum computing has the potential to be transformational in many sectors, including and not limited to cybersecurity, drug development and weather forecasting by releasing previously inaccessible computing power. Fundamentally, a quantum computer is able to perform an almost incomprehensible ($2^n$ for n qubits) number of calculations simultaneously, whilst a classical computer would have to work sequentially through them.
        In recent years quantum computing has made the transition from scientific research and theoretical study to physical realisations being built. Large companies such as IBM and Google are now leading the field \cite{boerkamp_quantum_2022}.
        This report reinforces the motivation for building a quantum computer and provides sufficient scientific background. It describes in detail two methods for building a quantum computer. One using trapped ions; a method that has successfully built small scale quantum computers and is fronted by companies such as IonQ and Microsoft. The other is linear optical quantum computing which is explored as a technique for developing the highly scaleable quantum computers of the future. This report provides an introduction to and consolidation of research explaining how, in a physical sense, to build a quantum computer. 

\end{abstract}
